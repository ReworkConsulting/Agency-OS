### 1.9 — Voice Rules

1. Never use corporate marketing language. If it sounds like it came from a template or a marketing agency, rewrite it. Use plain English that a contractor would use.
2. Lead with the problem, not the solution. Start by acknowledging what's broken or what the customer is frustrated with. The solution comes after you've earned credibility by understanding the pain.
3. Use specific numbers and timelines everywhere. "10 to 14 days," "six to seven hours," "6:30 a.m.," "$19 power bill," "31 panels," "410 watt," "100 milliseconds." Vagueness kills credibility. Specificity builds it.
4. Repeat key concepts in threes. "Transparency, honesty, and ethics." "It's simple. You live in the middle of the desert. It's easy. It's simple." This creates rhythm and memorability.
5. Use em-dashes for emphasis and asides. Don't hide important information in parentheses or separate sentences. Use em-dashes to integrate nuance while maintaining flow.
6. Speak from lived experience, not theory. Ground everything in what you've actually seen or done. "I got tired of..." not "One might observe that..."
7. Position solar as ownership and control, not just a utility upgrade. Reference autonomy, freedom from utility companies, and the ability to say "no" to power bills. Compare it to Tesla—cool, premium, and practical.
8. Use "show up" and "led from the front" language when talking about reliability and leadership. These are Spencer's core values. They should appear naturally in copy about the company's commitment.
9. Call out the competition without naming them. Reference "bad apples," "sales organizations," "used-car-like sales tactics," "deceptive reps," "complicated processes." Make it clear who you're not without being petty.
10. End with conviction or a call to action. Don't fade out. Leave the reader with a strong statement or a question that makes them think. "Stop procrastinating." "Give us a DM." "Show us your kilowatts."
11. Use "God, that was easy" as a testimonial anchor. This is the recurring client feedback. It should appear in copy, testimonials, and case studies. It's the proof that the process works.
12. If it sounds like AI-generated marketing copy, rewrite it. No "innovative solutions," no "transformative journey," no "unlock your potential." Stick to direct, human language that a real person would actually say.
13. Use humor to disarm skepticism. "If you're looking to hire us to install either solar or batteries and you're not planning on feeding us, please don't call us." Humor builds connection and makes the brand feel human.
14. Emphasize team loyalty and culture. "We're big on loyalty." "Here at Cooler Living, we're really focused on having our reps stick around for the long haul." This signals that the company cares about its people, which means it will care about customers too.
